Strategic context — the landscape this announcement lands in
This section is included because we believe it's important context for everyone involved in this decision — including leadership at the Live Nation level — to have a clear-eyed view of the community dynamics at play before this announcement goes out.
The perception challenge
A meaningful segment of the Bonnaroo community is aware that Live Nation owns C3 — and carries a latent concern that corporate ownership is eroding the festival's soul. This isn't a fringe view. It surfaces regularly in community threads and has compounded significantly across three specific events in the past 12 months. Camp & Ride going paid is the third in that sequence — which means the announcement is landing into an already sensitized audience.
▶ The three-strike context — click to expand
Strike 1 — The 2025 gift removal ("gift-gate")
For roughly 15 years, every Bonnaroo ticket came with a physical gift mailed to your door — in some years a lunchbox, always a bandana. The bandana was a cultural staple: worn at the farm, kept year-round, a daily countdown reminder. For 2025, Bonnaroo eliminated the gift entirely, citing rising costs and redirecting funds to infrastructure upgrades including the Infinity Stage and Outeroo bathrooms. The community immediately questioned whether a company backed by Live Nation really couldn't afford a bandana — and dubbed it "gift-gate." It became a dedicated episode of The What Podcast and generated significant Reddit thread activity. Most damaging detail: the bandana wasn't just removed — it was redirected to VIP-only camping tents in the new Area 931 premium zone, priced from $1,555 to $4,400. What was once a communal gift for every Roo became a decoration in luxury tents. The community saw this clearly as a monetization of tradition.
Strike 2 — The 2025 cancellation and 75% refund
Bonnaroo 2025 launched June 12 but was cancelled after just one day due to severe flooding. The initial response — offering only a 75% refund — prompted a 6,000-signature petition and widespread social media backlash explicitly naming Live Nation. Organizers reversed to 100% refunds under pressure. The sequence (cancellation → inadequate refund → public petition → reversal) reinforced the perception that the community had to fight for fair treatment from a corporate parent.
Strike 3 — Camp & Ride going paid
This announcement. A free service becoming a paid one, arriving after the above two events, will be processed through that accumulated lens before any individual facts are evaluated. The pedicab comparison and service improvement narrative are strong — but they need to cut through a community that is primed to see this as "another thing being taken away or monetized."
Why this matters for messaging
The announcement cannot afford to read like a corporate directive. Any language that sounds like it came from a Live Nation comms template — even inadvertently — will confirm the community's worst suspicions and create a harder PR problem than the price change itself. The draft copy in this document is deliberately written in C3's human voice for this reason. C3 is trusted. Live Nation as an entity is not, with this audience.
The opportunity inside the risk
Done well, this announcement is actually a chance to rebuild trust after 2024 — not just manage a price change. C3 stepping forward with transparency, a genuine explanation, and a fair deal signals that the people who built Bonnaroo are still making decisions that put Roos first. That's a story worth telling — and it's only credible coming from C3, not from a corporate parent.
The bottom line: The pricing change is defensible. The service improvement is real. The pedicab comparison is a genuine anchor. All of that works — but only if the voice delivering it is unmistakably C3's, not a corporate communications team's. We'd encourage anyone reviewing this document to ask: does this sound like Bonnaroo, or does it sound like a press release? The difference between those two things is the difference between a community that comes along and one that revolts.
Strategic analysis
Cutting the service entirely vs. transitioning to paid:
which is more painful for the community?
Based on community research across Reddit, the What Podcast, Extra Chill, and public C3 statements — here is our read on how each scenario would land.
Option A — Cut the service entirely
More painful
Eliminating Camp & Ride entirely would register as a permanent loss — not a policy change. The Bonnaroo community treats the tram as infrastructure, not a perk. Taking it away with no replacement would be processed the same way losing a stage or a campground plaza would be.
What the research shows
🔴 C3's own post-event survey specifically asked how much JamTrak affects attendees' experience — signaling they already know it's load-bearing for satisfaction scores.
🔴 The What Podcast interview with C3's Brad Parker revealed overall satisfaction scores after JamTrak launched were "some of the best we've ever had" — cutting it would directly reverse that.
🔴 The Farm is 700 acres. For attendees camped far from Centeroo, the tram isn't optional — it's access. Removing it effectively degrades the GA experience without refunding GA ticket prices.
🔴 In a community already raw about the 2024 cancellation and the 2025 ticket gift removal, eliminating another beloved amenity would land as a pattern — not an isolated decision.
Option B — Transition to paid service
Manageable — if handled right
A paid transition stings in the short term but preserves the service — and gives the community something to evaluate. The complaint becomes "it costs money now" rather than "it's gone." Those are very different conversations.
What the research shows
🟢 The What Podcast quote from a community member captured the prevailing attitude: "I don't feel like you guys have your hand in my wallet." That trust is conditional — and preserving the service, even for a fee, maintains that relationship better than removing it.
🟢 The pedicab anchor works precisely because a paid alternative already existed. The community accepted $15/ride as the market rate. Camp & Ride at $89 for five days is objectively better value than that status quo.
🟢 The 2025 ticket gift removal showed the community will grumble but absorb small losses if they believe the tradeoff is real. A visible, upgraded Camp & Ride service is the most tangible possible proof that the fee is justified.
🟢 Critically: if the service is genuinely better — shorter waits, smarter stops, more frequent trams — the on-site experience becomes the best argument for the price. Word of mouth at the festival itself does the work.
B
The verdict
Transitioning to a paid model is the significantly less painful option — provided the service quality genuinely improves and the announcement is handled with transparency. Cutting entirely would be perceived as a permanent downgrade to the GA experience, compounding on a year already marked by cancellation and reduced perks. The community will absorb a price before they'll absorb a loss.
The Live Nation dimension: Cutting the service would almost certainly be characterized in community spaces as Live Nation prioritizing margin over experience. A paid transition — framed correctly through C3's voice — can instead be characterized as C3 fighting to keep a service alive. That distinction is entirely a function of how it's announced and by whom.

Everything here is rough thinking, not a prescription. Take what works, ignore the rest, and make it sound like Bonnaroo — because your team knows that voice far better than we do.

04 — FAQ starting points
Draft answers to questions we'd anticipate from the community — your team will have a much better read on what Roos will actually ask. These are just our best guesses from spending time in the community research. Adapt, add to, or ignore as makes sense.
Camp & Ride — Frequently Asked Questions
Why isn't Camp & Ride free like JamTrak was?
Running on-site transportation as a free service was no longer sustainable operationally. Rather than let the service degrade or disappear, we brought in a dedicated professional transportation partner to run it properly. That requires a funded model. We negotiated the pricing to be as low as possible while ensuring the service is genuinely better than what came before.
Why does the festival pass make sense for me?
The festival pass is $89 pre-sale for unlimited rides all five days. A single walk-up ride is $15. Seven rides and the pass has paid for itself — and most Roos take far more than seven rides over a full weekend. If you're attending the full festival, the pass is almost certainly the right call.
What happened to the pedicabs?
Pedicabs have been retired from the Farm. They were charging $15 per ride with inconsistent pricing and unreliable service. Camp & Ride replaces them with a single standardized service — same walk-up price, but with professional operators, scheduled service, and accountability.
What if I only need the tram for one day?
The day pass is $45 for unlimited rides on a single day. If you're planning a big day — headliner night, long haul from camping — it breaks even at three rides and gives you freedom to hop on and off all day without thinking about it.
What's actually better about the new service?
A dedicated transportation partner whose only job is running Camp & Ride brings focus we couldn't provide while also producing a music festival. Expect more frequent trams, shorter waits, better information at pickup and drop-off stops, and consistent service throughout the day and night.
Can I buy a pass on-site if I didn't pre-purchase?
Yes — festival passes are available at the Farm for $109, day passes for $45, and single rides for $15 at any stop. Pre-purchasing saves you $20 on the festival pass and means you never have to think about it once you arrive.
Is there a limit on how many festival passes are available?
Pre-sale festival passes may be capped to ensure service levels remain high for everyone who purchases. If you want the $89 pre-sale price, don't wait.
05 — Community observations & response thinking
We spent some time in the Bonnaroo community trying to understand how this news might land. These are just our observations — your team knows this community far better than we do. We've sketched out some possible response approaches in case they're useful as a starting point, but your community managers will read these situations better in the moment.
What we noticed about the community
From spending time in the community across Reddit, Inforoo, and public posts, a few distinct personality types dominate the conversation. Two things stood out as critical context: (1) Bonnaroo ran an official post-2024 survey specifically asking how much JamTrak affected attendees' experience — meaning C3 already has data showing this service matters deeply; and (2) the 2024 cancellation has left the community in a fragile, slightly guarded place about the festival's future. This announcement lands into that context. Your team will know whether this maps to what you're seeing on the ground.
The Roo Veteran
10+ years, deeply protective of the community. Loudest voice on Reddit. Responds to honesty and history — not spin. Use "the Farm," "Bonnaroovians." Never sound corporate.
The Annual Crew
Goes with a large group, obsesses over logistics and cost. Calculating for 10+ people. Lead with the festival pass math — $89 vs. walk-up all weekend for a group is an obvious win.
The First-Timer
No reference for what the old JamTrak was. Just wants to know if it's worth it. Keep it forward-looking — professional service, easy to budget, one less thing to worry about on the Farm.
The Late Night Roo
Primary concern is hours, not price. Has feelings about the late-night service window. Hours are set for operational and insurance reasons — don't promise extensions that aren't coming. Reframe around frequency and reliability within the existing window instead.
The Sustainability Roo
Bikes, composts, cares about the Farm's footprint. One professional tram = fewer pedicabs + golf carts. Frame Camp & Ride as the green choice — this disarms "corporate greed" framing fast.
